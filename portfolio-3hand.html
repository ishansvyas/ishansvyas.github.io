Myoelectric Prosthetic Hand

A bionic hand controlled by muscle signals

Project Overview

Your brain uses electrical impulses to control your muscles. By attaching probes on your skin very close to your muscles, faint electrical signals can be picked up. We took those signals, amplified them, sent them into a computer made from scratch (see my MIPS CPU here), cleaned up the signal, analyzed it, and used the result to drive a motor controlling a separate 'Iron Man' hand.

More specifically, we used commercially available surface electromyography (sEMG) probes; created an Op-Amp circuit with differential amplification and a net gain of 1000; configured an ADC to interface with the CPU; wrote signal filtering, signal time-averaging, and servo control algorithms in MIPS assembly language; and constructed a 'prosthetic hand' fitted with a servo motor to control finger movement.

A photo of our prosthetic hand setup.

Background

Myoelectric prosthesis is an extensively explored area of research; a key difficulty in this project was in interfacing and configuring existing algorithms in assembly to work on a from-scratch CPU. We used this report from the company Delsys as a background on surface electromyography and EMG signal processing.
